Boozhoo, Aaniin. Welcome to the personal website for Zach Francis. This page contains a summary of my personal and professional experiences. To summarize myself, I am a father and husband; a half-Indian and half-white person; an archaeologist and a digital humanist. My daughter was born last year and my wife and I cannot get enough of her. History, upbringing, and daily life constantly remind me that I am a person walking in (at least) two worlds. And lastly, I spent the early part of my career trying to understand what it means to be an Indian through study. I have since figured out that understanding yourself doesn't come through your education or career. The skills I have learned in the attempt, however, will help me to fight for cultural heritage while also making it more accessible (when appropriate) for all of us trying to figure out what it means to be a human today.[Last Updated 7/01/2024]

Core Experiences

Archaeologist

UWO | MSU | Winsay, Inc | Verteba Cave | GVSU

I have extensive experience surveying, excavating, and processing cultural heritage materials. My first experiance was at a Grand Valley State University field School in 2014, where my class performed cultural resource managment for local sites in the area. For several years afterwards, I was privelaged enough to participate in archaeological excavations at Verteba Cave, located in Ukraine. I spent multiple summers and a winter hauling buckets of dirt out of a prolific cave dense with cultural materials. Before beginning graduate school in 2017, I also participated in cultural resource managment in Isabella County through a temporary contract with Winsay, Inc. In this project, I worked with several other archeaologists under Dr. Misty Jackson to survey a large deer hunting park using shovel test pits. While at MSU, I have participated in the campus archaeology program, which seeks to manage the cultural resouces on campus. I now currently work at the University of Wisconsin-Oshkosh as a project archaeologist in our AREL lab which is heavily involved with NAGPRA compliance.

August 2014 - Present

Digital Humanities

Digital Scholar | LEADR | Kora

See below for a list of my digital skills. I began my career in the digital humanities with the CHI Fellowship, where I made a simple digital repository and website for displaying cultural resources from the MSU anthropology department collections. Next came LEADR, which is a collaboration between the history and anthropology departments at Michigan State University which strives to help faculty, undergraduates and graduate students develop digital research projects. As a member of the LEADR staff, I have helped develop lesson plans, created asynchronous learning materials for digital tools, led guest lectures, and assisted students in the development of their digital research projects. Before the COVID-19 pandemic, I also helped with the day to day operations of the lab. Currently I work for Digital Scholar as the Omeka Training Specialist. I am responsible for the content and execution of the Omeka Intensives as well as any other training needs that our clients request (workshops, seminars, conference presentations, etc.)

Fall 2019 - Present

Anishinaabe

Life | SCIT Conservation Committee | SCIT Ziibiwing Center BOD | SCIT MIIBS Committee

My experience with being Ojibwe, with being Anishinaabe, started with my earliest memories. I remember dressing up for pow wows as a toddler, eating Indian Tacos, and selling crafts with my grandparents. I also remember being the only Indian kid in every grade of the private Catholic School that I attended. Despite the fond memories that I have in both of those worlds, it always felt like I was never fully part of either. An Indian to some, a pretendian to others. My experiences have led me to embrace this aspect of my life, rather than seeking justification and acceptance. In doing so, I have felt more accepted. I volunteer for a number of committees within my tribe, and I have even worked on a digital portal for quillworkers in the LTBB (see below).
